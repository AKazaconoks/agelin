## 401 vs 403: The Actor Model

The distinction is **authentication vs. authorization** — two different failure modes that happen to both look like "access denied" from the user's perspective.

### 401 Unauthorized — "I don't know who you are"

- **RFC 9110 definition**: "The request has not been applied because it lacks valid authentication credentials for the target resource."
- **Required**: A `WWW-Authenticate` response header must be included, telling the client *how* to authenticate.
- **Actor model**: The server is saying *"prove your identity"*. It cannot make an authorization decision yet. Re-authenticating might grant access.
- **Client behavior**: Should prompt for credentials and retry.

### 403 Forbidden — "I know who you are, but no"

- **RFC 9110 definition**: "The server understood the request but refuses to fulfill it."
- **Actor model**: The server has enough information to make a decision — and the decision is denial. This is a policy/authorization call, not an identity problem. Re-authenticating will not help.
- **Client behavior**: Should not retry; the request will not succeed regardless of credentials.

## Applied to Your Scenario

| Situation | Correct response |
|---|---|
| User is **not logged in** | **401** — identity unknown, prompt to authenticate |
| User is **logged in but lacks the required role/group** | **403** — identity known, access denied by policy |

---

## The Common Confusion

The names are historically misleading. "401 *Unauthorized*" is actually about **authentication** (unauthenticated), while "403 *Forbidden*" is about **authorization** (authenticated but not permitted). The spec authors acknowledged this awkward naming but kept it for backward compatibility.

**Bottom line**: 401 says *"who are you?"*; 403 says *"I know who you are — the answer is no."*
